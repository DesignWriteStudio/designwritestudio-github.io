Abstract:
The article presents an examination of the differences between various kinds of traditional and digital texts, as used within reading instruction in schools aligned to the U.S. Common Core State Standards. Topics addressed include an overview of the distinct features of traditional, interactive, and non-interactive digital texts; discussion of how to utilize each form for text complexity compliance; and their implications on balancing fiction and non-fiction in course material.

TECHNOLOGY in LITERACY INSTRUCTION

Over a decade ago, Harvey and Goudvis (2000) discussed the continued pressure placed on teachers to cover required curriculum, implement state mandates, and ensure students perform at high standards in all subject areas. Today, the implementation of the Common Core State Standards (CCSS, 2010) is currently one of the newly added pressures and a main topic of educational conversations. Not only are educators concerned with the overall implementation of the CCSS, but also understanding and effectively translating text complexity (Hiebert & Mesmer, 2013) in teaching and learning. When attempting to interpret the goal of Reading Standard 10, much attention should be placed on how students should read and comprehend texts of various types and complexity levels, including informational and technical texts in content areas. As teachers attempt to implement Reading Standard 10, this could be an opportune time to explore digital text. For the purpose of this column, I will begin with an overview of traditional and digital text (non-interactive and interactive). Next, I will provide information of how utilizing digital texts and productivity resources can automatically vary the complexity of the text. Finally, I will address measurements of complex text for both print and digital text. This section will conclude with my final thoughts of why electronic texts are valuable in balancing fiction and nonfiction texts in the content classroom.

Traditional Text

In many classrooms today, students are accustomed to reading textbooks, trade books, and even articles from the newspaper or a magazine. All of these print sources provide students with access to various reading genres and as the text becomes more complex, students will be required to use more strategies.

When educators think about the tasks involved with reading, they realize print text is "linear, static, temporally and physically bounded, often with clear purpose, authorship and authority" (Dalton & Proctor, 2009, p. 297). All of these characteristics are important to understand when teachers choose texts for students to read. It is also an important part in understanding why different texts are chosen when teachers consider the students' purpose for reading. Teachers need to be more deliberate in the nonfiction text they are choosing for students to read. The Core Knowledge Foundation (2013) identifies the need to "carefully choose nonfiction content in knowledge domains that builds background knowledge, grow vocabulary, and invites deeper exploration over time" (unpaged). Teachers can accomplish this by selecting quality literature and content rich topics as part of their routine read-aloud lessons.

Non-interactive Digital Text

When making the transition from traditional print to the digital text, Wolsey (2012a) claims "readers must understand the fundamental differences of reading electronic texts from those in the paper-based environment, and they must be able to regulate comprehension processes in ways that recognize these differences" (unpaged). In addition, Wyatt-Smith and Elkins (2009) identify comprehension in the digital world as the new literacies of online reading comprehension. They claim the complexity of reading digital text more overwhelming and more integrating of reading comprehension processes than print reading. They continue the conversation by noting that as education shifts from a print world to a three-dimensional digital space, readers are required to orient themselves in a new way as learners.

In this article, non-interactive digital text refers to text that is essentially copied and pasted into the digital environment. This could be a linear text such as, PDF, or an e-book. This type of text does not have any hyperlinks, associated productivity tools, or even distracting advertisements on the page. Although it may seem that reading this type of text involves the same processes as print text, it does not. However, an advantage to this type of text, in comparison to print text, is the additional assistance non-interactive texts can provide. Some e-books, for example, provide a read aloud feature for students. This becomes a type of built-in scaffolding for readers who may not be fluent at the higher reading level. In addition, utilizing this type of text can be a stepping stone for teachers to begin teaching about how to read digital text. According to Wolsey (2012c), when reading online texts, the eye moves in an "F" shape. Readers begin by taking in "key terms most often on the left side of the screen and sweeps across in some places forming the arms of the 'F'" (Wolsey, 2012c, unpaged). Knowing this information is helpful to educators because the basic teaching practices teach students to read from left-to-right. If educators teach students about the natural "F" pattern the eye tends to make, this can allow students to understand the difference between reading online text and print text. These types of print look different, so readers need to view them differently as well.

On the other hand, Bauerlein's (2011) article titled Too Dumb for Complex Texts (2011) claims that digital texts and tools "pull young minds in one direction, digital diversions in another" (unpaged). When considering text complexity, Bauerlein identifies three key dispositions of readers in order to be successful -- a willingness to probe, the capacity for uninterrupted thinking, and receptivity to deep thinking. The article continues to point out difficulties students encounter when reading complex texts: the need to understand ideas in one paragraph before moving on, increased vocabulary that needs to be understood, and the importance of re-reading to deepen comprehension of the text.

Interactive Digital Text

Interactive digital text is "nonlinear, multimodal, interactive and unbound in time and space" (Dalton & Proctor, 2009, p. 297). Therefore, when considering comprehension of digital text (Coiro, 2003; Dalton & Proctor, 2009; Wolsey, 2012a), it can be argued that if we increase access to digital environments, students will gain skills needed to comprehend complex texts. Coiro argues how reading digital text requires the application of familiar processes and skills, such as recognizing main ideas and making inferences, in conjunction with new processes required to read digital text formats, such as evaluating hyperlink information to guide strategic searching and critically evaluating the credibility and relevance of Web information (as cited in Coiro, Kno-bel, Lankshear, & Leu, 2009, p. 303).

Moreover, Wolsey (2012b) also identifies the need for teaching students strategies that can be used when reading digital text. Wolsey's model for comprehending difficult texts in the digital environment is the MNOP strategy. It involves the practices of modeling, naming, overcoming obstacles, and probing, in which each of these strategies is required to comprehend any text. The propose MNOP strategy is to provide students with the tools to find, engage, and comprehend materials within the digital world.

Another strategy for increasing comprehension of texts is to pair fiction and non-fiction texts (Soalt, 2005). This strategy ultimately increases background knowledge and vocabulary for the subject, which will later assist in reading more complex texts on the same subject. Frye, Trathen, and Wilson's (2009) article "Pirates in historical fiction and nonfiction: A twin-text unit of study" provides an example of a twin-text unit of study. The pairing of fiction and nonfiction print and/ or digital texts is an effective method for students to make connections between how narrative and expository texts present similar topics and information in different ways.

Digital Text and Productivity Resources

Text complexity involves giving the students a wider array of text (e.g., fiction, nonfiction, graphic novels, digital resources, etc.). When utilizing digital resources to enhance text types, teachers will provide opportunities for their students to transfer skills from one text to another. These skills may range from vocabulary and syntax to the development of writing an opinion piece. They can also extend the connections made between the texts in the classroom and the digital resources to which they may make connections. For example, students studying a unit on frogs may read a fiction story about a girl and her pet frog, a nonfiction story providing facts about frogs, and then decide to use online resources to map the location of various frog species they learned from their reading. In addition, they can use tools like Diigo (www.diigo.com) and Markup (http://markup.io/) for writing comments and notes directly on the webpages for their teacher or group members. Teachers can even set up a site like Ponder (www.pon-deri.ng) to make an online text become interactive with the entire class. Teachers can pose questions about specific parts of the reading, and then students select an answer to demonstrate their understanding. Measures of Complex Text

The level of text complexity must be considered when using digital or print text with students. The Common Core State Standards Appendix A: Research Supporting the Key Elements of the Standards provides three models for determining the complexity of text. The models are quantitative and qualitative dimensions, and reader and task consideration [http://wwwcorestan-dards.org/ELA-Literacy/standard-10-range-quality-complexity/measuring-text-complexity-three-factors]. These models are used to assist teachers in making sound professional decisions of matching reader to text.

As teachers increase the use of digital text, a quantitative measure, known as ATOS Analyzer (ATOS stands for Advantage/TASA Open Standard) is useful for analysis of books and digital text (i.e., online articles or passages). This tool is in an easy to use format for educators. Other useful online sites for analyzing books are as follows:

* the ksde.org/kscommoncore website

* The state of Kansas website http://kansas. bookconnect.com/default.aspx

* Scholastic's Book Wizard leveling system http://bookwizard.scholastic.com

Clearly, there are variations in ways traditional or digital complex text can be assessed to determine and match the book to the reader.

Final Thoughts

The current norm with reading instruction has teachers focused on using primarily fiction texts in the classroom. With the change in direction that education is taking with the push from Common Core State Standards and the demand for students to understand the digital world, teachers will need to shift to a balance between fiction and nonfiction text using both traditional and digital texts. Based on research and my own classroom experiences with digital text, students find working in digital environments exciting and engaging. In addition, it has become a necessity. People living in today's world are constantly utilizing technology tools to accomplish everyday tasks. Therefore, as teachers strive to balance fiction and nonfiction content in the classroom, they should move forward with incorporating digital text complexity to assist students in reading a variety of genres. In sum, not only are teachers currently faced with the task of providing students with experiences with more complex texts and text types, but also have the task of helping students see the digital world and the physical world we live in, as a cohesive unit.
